Data & Methodology

How we sourced, verified, and documented 874+ historical signs and exhibits flagged or removed from America's National Parks and Wildlife Refuges.

Transparency matters. This project makes extraordinary claims backed by leaked government data. We owe you a detailed accounting of where every data point comes from, how we verified it, and what we still don't know.

Primary Data Sources
🗄️
Internal NPS Review Database
Primary Source — Leaked Government Data
PrimaryVerified

Internal NPS database cataloging every interpretive sign, wayside exhibit, and informational panel across the National Park System. The leak included park names, sign titles, text content, GPS coordinates, and internal review status flags.

This database is the foundation of the project. Every entry on the map traces back to a record in this export.

📋
Save Our Signs (SOS) Tracker
Cross-Reference Source
SecondaryVerified

A community-maintained spreadsheet at saveoursigns.org that tracks removal/revision status of park signs, including before/after photos, press citations, and status updates from park visitors.

We cross-reference SOS status columns to confirm or update our removal/revision classifications. Photo evidence from SOS is credited as "Sierra Club exhibit documentation" where applicable.

🕸️
Wayback Machine Archives
Archival Verification
Verification

For entries where NPS web pages have been modified or taken down, we provide Wayback Machine links as permanent archival evidence of the original content.

All Wayback URLs have been audited to confirm they resolve to the correct snapshot.

📰
Press Reporting & FOIA
Supplementary Sources
Secondary

Investigative reporting from Outside Magazine, Condé Nast Traveler, Washington Post, and others. FOIA requests for U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service interpretive materials supplement the NPS data with FWS-specific entries.

Verification Process

Every entry undergoes the following checks before publication:

1. Park name and location audit. We verify each park name against the official NPS unit list and confirm the state assignment matches. Entries with mismatched states (e.g., Theodore Roosevelt NP originally listed as MT instead of ND) are corrected and logged.

2. Narrative cross-check. We verify that the narrative describes the correct park. Early in the project we found entries where Rock Creek Park's narrative described Rocky Mountain NP — every narrative has been reviewed for this class of error.

3. Status confirmation. We cross-reference the leaked database status with the SOS Tracker's independently maintained status column. Where they disagree, we default to the most recently verified status.

4. Photo validation. All photo URLs are tested for resolution. Broken image references are removed. Photos are tagged with source attribution (NPS internal, Sierra Club/SOS, press, visitor-contributed).

5. Wayback link audit. Every Wayback Machine URL is tested to confirm it resolves to the correct archival snapshot of the relevant NPS page.

Known Limitations

This is not a complete record. The leaked database may not capture every removal or revision, particularly those that occurred before the leak or at sites not yet cataloged in the internal NPS review database.

GPS coordinates are park-level centroids, not the exact sign location. Multiple entries at the same park share the same coordinates.

Status may lag reality. Signs may have been removed or restored since our last data update. We rely on community reports and press coverage to stay current.

FWS entries are less complete than NPS entries. The FOIA-sourced Fish & Wildlife Service data covers fewer fields and has fewer photographs.

Update Frequency

The dataset is updated on a rolling basis as new removals are confirmed, court orders restore content, or community members submit photo evidence. Major updates are committed to the project's git repository with descriptive commit messages.
