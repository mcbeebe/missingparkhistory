Secretary’s Order 3431

The Interior Department policy that launched a systematic censorship campaign across America’s national parks, wildlife refuges, and public lands. Here’s what it says, what it does, and how it’s being challenged.

What It Says

Secretary’s Order 3431 is the implementing directive for Executive Order 14253. While the executive order set the broad policy direction, SO 3431 is the operational order that told agencies what to do, when, and how.

At its core, the order directs all Interior Department land management agencies to review every piece of public-facing interpretive content — signs, exhibits, museum displays, brochures, audio tours, and digital media — and to identify and remove any content that falls into two categories:

“Images, descriptions, depictions, messages, narratives or other information (content) that inappropriately disparages Americans past or living (including persons living in colonial times), or, with respect to content describing natural features, that emphasizes matters unrelated to the beauty, abundance, or grandeur of said natural feature.” — Secretary’s Order 3431, Section 4 (May 20, 2025)

In practice, this language has been used to target four main categories of content: historical accounts of slavery at plantation sites, climate science at parks experiencing environmental change, Indigenous history and displacement narratives, and LGBTQ+ history at memorial and heritage sites.

Key Provisions
Provision 1
Content Review Mandate
Directs all Interior bureaus — NPS, BLM, USFWS, and Bureau of Reclamation — to conduct a comprehensive review of all public-facing interpretive materials. Sets deadlines for agencies to identify content flagged for removal or revision.
Provision 2
“Disparagement” Standard
Establishes a vague and subjective standard for what constitutes content that “inappropriately disparages Americans past or living, including persons living in colonial times.” In practice, this has been interpreted to include factual accounts of slaveholders, colonists, and historical figures involved in forced displacement.
Provision 3
Natural Feature Restriction
Restricts interpretive content at natural sites to topics related to the “beauty, abundance, or grandeur” of natural features. This provision has been used to justify removing climate science exhibits from parks like Glacier, Acadia, and Fort Sumter — even when the science directly explains changes visitors can see.
Provision 4
Monument Reinstatement Review
Directs agencies to review any monuments, memorials, statues, or markers that were altered or removed since January 1, 2020, with potential reinstatement if changes are deemed inconsistent with the administration’s interpretation of history.
Provision 5
250th Anniversary Priority
Mandates the NPS complete infrastructure improvements at Independence National Historical Park ahead of the 250th anniversary of the signing of the Declaration of Independence in 2026.
The Chain of Authority

Understanding how this censorship campaign was authorized requires following the chain of directives from the White House to individual park sites:

Document Date What It Does
Executive Order 14253 March 27, 2025 Sets the policy: directs Interior Department to review how American history is portrayed at federal sites
Secretary’s Order 3431 May 20, 2025 Implements EO 14253: sets specific directives, deadlines, and content standards for removal
NPS Internal Directives Summer 2025 Operationalizes SO 3431: park superintendents instructed to review content using the “Wayside Machine” database
Park-Level Removals 2025–present Individual parks flag, revise, and remove content. 874+ entries documented across 120+ sites.

The “Wayside Machine” is the NPS’s internal database used to catalog every interpretive sign, exhibit, and wayside marker in the park system. Under SO 3431, park staff used this database to flag content for removal. When the database leaked publicly in 2025, it became the primary source for documenting the censorship campaign — and the backbone of this project’s interactive map.

What Historians & Experts Say

The order has drawn sharp criticism from historians, scientists, and conservation organizations. The Organization of American Historians (OAH), the largest professional society for scholars of American history, issued a formal statement condemning SO 3431 as “censorship of history in the National Park Service.”

The National Trust for Historic Preservation warned that the order threatens the integrity of historic interpretation across the entire federal system. A national coalition including the Sierra Club called on Secretary Burgum to rescind the order entirely.

NPS staff themselves have raised concerns. Internal records show that park professionals flagged some content as “factually accurate” yet were still directed to remove it under the order’s subjective “disparagement” standard.

Legal Challenges

SO 3431 is facing its most significant legal challenge in NPCA et al. v. Department of the Interior, filed February 17, 2026 by a coalition represented by Democracy Forward. The six plaintiff organizations are:

National Parks Conservation Association · Association of National Park Rangers · Coalition to Protect America’s National Parks · American Association for State and Local History · Union of Concerned Scientists · Society for Experiential Graphic Design

The lawsuit argues that the removal directives are “arbitrary and capricious and contrary to law” under the Administrative Procedure Act. It names Interior Secretary Doug Burgum and acting NPS Director Jessica Bowron as defendants.

In a related action, Federal District Judge Cynthia M. Rufe ordered the NPS to restore exhibits that had been removed from the President’s House Site at Independence NHP, ruling that the removal likely violated federal law. The exhibits — which examined the paradox between slavery and freedom in the nation’s founding — were restored on February 19, 2026.

In March 2026, the coalition filed a motion for preliminary injunction seeking to halt all ongoing removals and compel restoration of all previously removed exhibits while the case proceeds.

The legal fight is ongoing. For a broader overview of the censorship campaign and what’s being removed, see our NPS Censorship explainer. To see the real-world impact at specific parks, explore the interactive map.

Executive Order 14253 — The Parent Directive

SO 3431 implements Executive Order 14253, “Restoring Truth and Sanity to American History,” signed by President Trump on March 27, 2025. The executive order frames its directive as a response to what it calls an effort “to rewrite the nation’s history, replacing objective facts with a distorted narrative.”

EO 14253 directs the Department of the Interior to determine whether, since January 1, 2020, any monuments, memorials, statues, and markers within its jurisdiction contain content that “inappropriately disparages Americans past or living.” It also orders the reinstatement of any materials removed since 2020. The Smithsonian Institution is separately directed to review its museums, research centers, and the National Zoo.

The official text is published in the Federal Register and archived by the American Presidency Project at UCSB.
