Entry #720 CONFIRMED REMOVED

CONFIRMED REMOVED: Exhibit panels, publications (3 items)

Exhibit panels, publications (3 items) 1 photo

In 1872, the Grand Tetons' dramatic peaks rising 13,000 feet from the valley floor captured the imagination of conservationists who eventually protected them as Grand Teton National Park in 1929.

Grand Teton Mountain reaches 13,775 feet and dominates the Teton Range, which rises abruptly from the floor of Jackson Hole. The park was created after fierce local opposition; Jackson Hole National Monument was created in 1943 and later merged into the park. Contains pristine glacial lakes, abundant wildlife, and some of the most dramatic mountain scenery in North America.

From the internal review records: “In January 1870, Doane participated in what is now known as the Marias Massacre, at which, the U.S. Army killed over 170 Piegan Blackfeet, including many women, elders, and children. Doane wrote fondly about this attack and bragged about it for the rest of his life. "”

Why this matters: Grand Tetons showcase the raw power of mountain geology—where uplift and erosion created one of Earth's most dramatic landscapes. Over 3.3 million people visit this site each year.

Text asks visitors to examine how we tell history of a person. Doane was involved in early expeditions of YELL/GRTE and involved in massacre of Blackfeet tribal members.

"Heading:

How do we acknowledge the good and bad of a historic figure?

Gustavus Cheyney Doane is a challenging figure. While he is known to many for his explorations and national park contributions, many Indigenous people know him for his connection to the deaths of their ancestors.

Doane served in the U.S. Army and participated in several expeditions in the greater Yellowstone area. His detailed journal notes and reports (including the quote shown here) played a role in future decisions to establish national parks in this area.

In January 1870, Doane participated in what is now known as the Marias Massacre, at which, the U.S. Army killed over 170 Piegan Blackfeet, including many women, elders, and children. Doane wrote fondly about this attack and bragged about it for the rest of his life. "
